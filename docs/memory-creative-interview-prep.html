Key insight for the interview: The pipeline isn't about replacing creatives. Stage 1
Brief Intake & Parsing

From messy client input to structured creative brief

Clients send briefs in every format — emails, PDFs, Slack messages, decks with 47 slides. The Brief Parser Agent takes unstructured input and extracts a structured creative brief that the rest of the pipeline can work with.

What the agent extracts

  • Objective: What's the campaign trying to achieve? (awareness, conversion, engagement, retention)
  • Audience: Who are we talking to? Demographics, psychographics, behavioral segments
  • Channels: Where does this run? (LinkedIn, Instagram, TikTok, CTV, OOH, email)
  • Tone & voice: Playful? Authoritative? Irreverent? Mapped to brand voice guidelines
  • Constraints: Budget, timeline, legal/regulatory requirements, mandatory inclusions
  • Deliverables: What assets are needed? (hero video, 6 social cuts, 3 email variants, landing page)
  • Success metrics: What does "good" look like? (CTR target, view-through rate, engagement benchmarks)

How it actually works in practice

The agent uses an LLM (Claude or GPT-4o) with a system prompt loaded with the agency's brief template. It parses the client input, flags anything missing ("no target audience specified — defaulting to brand's primary persona"), and outputs a structured JSON brief that feeds into the next stages.

If it's a returning client, the agent also pulls prior campaign briefs from the knowledge base to maintain continuity — "last campaign targeted 25-34 urban professionals, same audience assumed unless specified otherwise."

"The brief is the contract between the client and the creative team. If you get the brief wrong, everything downstream is wrong. An AI agent can parse a messy 12-page RFP into a structured brief in minutes, flag gaps, and pull context from prior campaigns — but the creative director still signs off before anything moves to ideation. That human gate is non-negotiable."

Stage 2 — Research & Context Building

Grounding every output in real data

This is where RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) earns its keep. Before any creative work starts, the Research Agent assembles the context package that will inform every generation downstream.

What gets pulled

  • Brand guidelines: Logo usage, color palette, typography, photography style, tone-of-voice rules, do's and don'ts
  • Previous campaigns: What worked, what didn't, performance data from prior creative
  • Competitor landscape: What competitors are running right now (scraped or uploaded), creative positioning gaps
  • Cultural/trend context: Trending topics, seasonal relevance, platform algorithm shifts (e.g., "Instagram is prioritizing Reels over static in Q2")
  • Audience insights: CRM data, survey results, social listening data, persona docs
  • Legal/compliance rules: Industry-specific disclaimers, claim substantiation requirements, rights and clearances

The RAG architecture

Brand documents are chunked (typically 500-1000 tokens per chunk), embedded using a model like text-embedding-3-large, and stored in a vector database (pgvector, Pinecone, Weaviate). When a brief comes in, the agent queries the vector store with the brief's key terms and retrieves the top-k most relevant chunks.

Critical detail: brand guidelines need to be structured, not just dumped in. A 200-page brand book should be broken into discrete sections (voice, visual, messaging pillars, audience personas) with metadata tags so retrieval is precise. "Retrieve all voice guidelines for the enterprise audience segment" is very different from "retrieve everything."

"RAG is what separates generic AI content from brand-consistent output. Every generation prompt gets injected with relevant brand context before the model sees it. But the quality of your RAG depends entirely on how you structure and chunk your brand documents. A 200-page brand book dumped as one file is useless — you need it broken into tagged, queryable sections. Voice guidelines for enterprise vs consumer, visual rules for social vs print, messaging pillars per persona. That's the unsexy work that makes the creative output actually good."

Stage 3 — Ideation & Concepting

Generating creative directions at speed

This is where the Creative Director Agent (or a team of agents) generates multiple concept directions. The goal isn't to replace the human CD — it's to give them 10 directions to react to instead of starting from a blank page.

How multi-agent ideation works

  • Divergent generation: 3-5 "concepting agents" each generate ideas from different angles — one focused on emotional storytelling, one on humor, one on data-driven proof points, one on cultural relevance
  • Concept cards: Each agent outputs a structured concept card — headline, visual direction, key copy lines, tone rationale, platform adaptation notes
  • Selector/Ranker agent: Reviews all concepts against the brief's objectives and brand guidelines, scores them on relevance, originality, and feasibility, ranks top 3-5
  • Human gate: Creative Director reviews ranked concepts, picks 1-2 to develop, provides direction notes ("love direction B but make the tone less corporate, more like how we did the CFL work")

The parallel vs sequential decision

Parallel generation runs multiple concepting agents simultaneously — faster, more diverse, but uses more compute. Sequential generation has one agent build on the previous agent's ideas — more cohesive but slower and risks echo-chamber thinking. Best practice: parallel for initial divergence, sequential for refinement once a direction is chosen.

"Ideation is where AI is most misunderstood. People think it's about the AI having the idea. It's not. It's about giving the creative director 10 directions to react to in 20 minutes instead of the team spending two days in a brainstorm to get 3. The CD still picks the winner, still shapes the direction — but they're reacting and editing instead of generating from zero. That's a fundamentally different creative process, and it's faster without being worse."

Stage 4 — Production & Asset Generation

Where agents actually make things

Once a concept direction is approved, production agents execute. This is the most tool-heavy stage — different agents for copy, image, video, audio, and layout.

Copywriter Agent
Long-form & Short-form Copy

Generates headlines, body copy, CTAs, social captions, email subject lines. System prompt loaded with brand voice + brief context via RAG. Outputs multiple variants per asset for A/B testing.

Designer Agent
Image & Visual Generation

Calls Midjourney/Firefly/DALL-E APIs with structured prompts: style references + negative prompts + brand color constraints. Generates hero images, social graphics, ad creative variants.

Video Agent
Motion & Video Production

Runway Gen-4.5 for video generation from stills or text, ElevenLabs for voiceover, automated cuts for platform-specific aspect ratios (16:9, 9:16, 1:1).

Layout Agent
Composition & Formatting

Assembles copy + visuals into final layouts per channel spec. Auto-generates responsive email templates, social post graphics with text overlay, landing page sections.

The production workflow in detail

Production isn't linear — it's a DAG (directed acyclic graph). Copy and visuals generate in parallel. Once both are ready, the Layout Agent composites them. Video agents may depend on both still images (as starting frames) and copy (as script/voiceover text).

Example for a social campaign:

// Production DAG for a social campaign Brief (approved concept #2) ├── Copywriter Agent │ ├── 3x headline variants │ ├── 3x body copy variants │ ├── 6x social captions (2 per platform) │ └── 2x email subject lines │ ├── Designer Agent // runs in parallel with copy │ ├── 1x hero image (16:9) │ ├── 3x social variants (1:1, 4:5, 9:16) │ └── 2x style explorations │ ├── Video Agent // depends on hero image + copy │ ├── 1x 30s hero video │ ├── 3x 6-15s social cuts │ └── 1x voiceover (ElevenLabs) │ └── Layout Agent // depends on copy + visuals ├── Social post composites (copy + image overlays) ├── Email template (header image + copy blocks) └── Landing page hero section

Style propagation — the hard part

The biggest challenge in multi-agent production is visual consistency. Each image generation call is independent — the model doesn't "remember" the previous image. Solutions:

  • Style reference images: Feed approved brand imagery as style references to every generation call
  • Seed locking: Use consistent random seeds for similar compositions
  • Fine-tuned LoRAs: Train a lightweight style adapter on the client's existing creative. 500-2,000 curated examples is the sweet spot
  • Negative prompts: Explicitly exclude off-brand elements ("no stock photo look, no neon colors, no serif fonts")
  • Adobe Firefly Foundry: Enterprise custom model trained on client's own brand assets — commercially safe, brand-locked

"Production is where you need to think in DAGs, not linear pipelines. Copy and visuals generate in parallel — there's no reason to wait. But video depends on both, and layout depends on everything. The orchestration framework needs to understand those dependencies. LangGraph handles this well because you define nodes and edges explicitly, and it manages the execution order. CrewAI is simpler but works for linear flows. The real challenge isn't orchestration though — it's style consistency across generated assets. Every image call is stateless. You solve that with style references, seed locking, and ideally a LoRA fine-tuned on the client's existing creative."

Stage 5 — Review, QA & Brand Compliance

The quality gate that prevents disasters

Before any human sees the final creative, a Brand Compliance Agent runs automated QA. This is where agencies protect themselves and their clients.

What the compliance agent checks

  • Brand voice alignment: LLM-as-judge scores copy against brand voice guidelines (retrieved via RAG). "Does this sound like [brand]?" scored 1-5 with rationale
  • Visual brand check: Color palette compliance (hex values within approved range), logo usage rules, photography style match
  • Legal/regulatory: Required disclaimers present, claims substantiated, rights/clearances verified, industry-specific rules (e.g., pharma fair balance, financial disclosures)
  • Accessibility: Alt text present, color contrast ratios meet WCAG 2.1, video captions generated
  • Platform specs: Image dimensions correct per platform, video duration within limits, character counts within bounds
  • Factual accuracy: Claims cross-referenced against source data, statistics verified, dates/names checked

Risk-based routing

Not every piece of creative needs the same level of review. Smart pipelines route based on risk:

  • Low risk (internal blog post, organic social) → Auto-approved if compliance score > 4.0/5.0
  • Medium risk (paid social, email campaign) → Junior reviewer + compliance pass
  • High risk (TV/CTV, regulated industry, anything with legal claims) → Senior CD review + legal sign-off required

"Brand compliance isn't a nice-to-have, it's the thing that keeps the agency from getting fired. An AI compliance agent can check 50 assets in the time it takes a human to check 2 — color accuracy, voice alignment, legal disclaimers, platform specs. But the key is risk-based routing. Not everything needs the CCO's eyes. A social post that scores 4.5/5 on brand voice and passes all compliance checks can auto-approve. A regulated industry TV spot needs human review no matter what. The agent handles the volume, the humans handle the judgment calls."

Stage 5b — Revision Loop

Incorporating feedback without restarting

When a reviewer (human or agent) flags issues, the Revision Agent incorporates feedback and regenerates — without restarting the entire pipeline.

How the revision loop works

  • Reviewer provides structured feedback: what's wrong, what to change, reference for what "right" looks like
  • Revision Agent receives original generation context + feedback as a new prompt
  • Only the flagged assets regenerate — approved assets are locked and don't re-enter the loop
  • Max iteration bound: Pipeline caps at 3 revision rounds before escalating to human ("we've revised this 3 times and compliance still flags the tone — needs creative director input")
  • Full revision history is tracked — every version, every feedback note, every score change

State management is critical here. The pipeline state object carries the brief, brand context, all generated assets, all feedback, all scores, and revision history. LangGraph's typed state and checkpointing handles this natively — you can "rewind" to any point in the pipeline and branch from there. CrewAI doesn't have this built in, which is why LangGraph is better for complex creative workflows.

Stage 6 — Multi-Channel Publishing

One brief, every channel, formatted correctly

The Distribution Agent takes approved final creative and formats, resizes, and delivers to each channel's specifications.

What the distribution agent handles

  • Auto-formatting: Hero image → Instagram square (1:1), Instagram Story (9:16), LinkedIn (1.91:1), Twitter (16:9), email header (600px wide)
  • Copy adaptation: Full blog post → LinkedIn summary (3,000 chars max) → Twitter thread (280/tweet) → Instagram caption (2,200 chars) → email preview text (90 chars)
  • Video renditions: 30s hero → 15s Instagram Reel → 6s bumper ad → square format for feed
  • DAM upload: Final assets tagged with metadata (campaign, client, date, channel, format, version) and uploaded to Digital Asset Management system (Aprimo, AEM, Bynder)
  • CMS/scheduler push: Content delivered to publishing tools (Hootsuite, HubSpot, WordPress) with scheduled publish times per channel

"The least glamorous part of the pipeline is where agencies burn the most hours. Taking one hero asset and manually resizing it for 8 platforms, rewriting the caption for each character limit, exporting 4 aspect ratios of the same video — that's pure production labor. A distribution agent does that in seconds. And it tags everything with proper metadata so the DAM isn't a mess 6 months later. That's where AI saves the most time in a real agency workflow."

Architecture Deep Dive

Agent Orchestration Patterns

There are four core patterns for how agents work together. Know these — they'll come up in any system design question.

Pattern How It Works Best For
Sequential Pipeline Agent A → Agent B → Agent C. Output of each feeds the next. Simple, predictable, easy to debug. Linear workflows (brief → copy → review). Most common in production.
Parallel + Merge Multiple agents run simultaneously, a Selector agent picks the best output or merges results. Ideation (3 concepts at once), A/B variant generation, multi-format production.
Supervisor / CD Pattern A "Creative Director" agent delegates tasks to specialist agents, reviews their output, requests revisions, assembles final deliverable. Complex campaigns with multiple asset types. The supervisor maintains creative coherence.
Critic Loop Generator produces → Critic scores against rubric → Generator revises → repeat until threshold or max iterations. Quality-sensitive outputs. Brand voice refinement, compliance-heavy content.

In practice, you combine them. A real campaign pipeline uses Parallel+Merge for ideation, Sequential for production, Critic Loop for QA, and a Supervisor overseeing the whole thing. LangGraph lets you compose these patterns because it's graph-based — each pattern is a subgraph wired into the larger pipeline.

Enterprise Reality

The Gap Between Testing and Deploying

The McKinsey Number

~90% of CMOs are testing AI. Fewer than 10% have deployed end-to-end workflows at scale.

The bottleneck isn't model capability. It's governance, interoperability, and organizational readiness.

What blocks enterprise deployment

  • Brand/legal governance: No framework for who approves AI-generated content, what the liability is, how to audit
  • System interoperability: AI tools don't connect to existing DAM, CMS, CRM, approval workflows. Manual handoffs everywhere
  • Skills gap: Creatives know design, not prompt engineering. Engineers know code, not brand. The bridge role barely exists yet
  • Data readiness: Brand guidelines aren't machine-readable. Campaign performance data lives in 6 different tools. No single source of truth
  • Measurement: No agreed-on framework for evaluating AI creative quality. "Is this good?" is still vibes-based

"This is where an agency like Memory has an unfair advantage. You're not trying to deploy AI inside a 50,000-person enterprise with 18 approval layers. You're a small, composable team that can ship a pipeline, test it on a real campaign, and iterate in a week. The Adobe-WPP-NVIDIA stack is the reference architecture for the Unilevers of the world, but most brands will access creative AI through their agency partners first. The agency becomes the AI-enabled layer."

Connecting the Dots

What Memory Creative Probably Wants to Hear

Their Context

Ian was McCann's Global Creative AI Lead. He's not asking "should we use AI?" — he's asking "how do we build the infrastructure to use it at agency scale?"

Topics to lead with

  • Composable pipelines: Memory's team model is composable (contractors per project). Their AI pipeline should be too — modular agents that swap in/out based on the campaign type
  • Brand context as infrastructure: Every client gets a structured knowledge base. Voice guidelines, visual rules, messaging pillars, competitor intelligence — all queryable via RAG. This is the reusable asset that makes every campaign faster
  • Human-AI collaboration model: Agents handle volume and variation. Humans handle taste and judgment. The pipeline enforces this with explicit approval gates — not optional, built into the graph
  • Production economics: A campaign that takes a team of 6 three weeks to produce — research, concepting, copywriting, design, video, trafficking — can compress to 3-5 days with a well-built pipeline. The agency doesn't cut headcount; they take on more clients at the same team size
  • Multi-provider routing: No single AI provider covers the full creative pipeline. You need Azure for image/video gen, Vertex for music, Bedrock for editing tools, Anthropic for compliance. A gateway that routes to the right model per task is the infrastructure layer
  • Measurement from day one: Every generated asset gets scored automatically. First-pass rate, revision rounds, human edit time. This data improves the pipeline over time and proves ROI to clients

Things NOT to say

  • "AI will replace creatives" — Ian has 31 Cannes Lions. He knows the value of human creativity. Frame AI as leverage, not replacement
  • "Just use ChatGPT" — they know. They want infrastructure, not a chatbot
  • "Fine-tune everything" — prompt engineering + RAG handles 90% of cases. Fine-tuning is expensive and only worth it for very specific style needs
  • Overcomplicating the stack — a working pipeline with 3 agents beats a theoretical pipeline with 15
  • "Seedance for video" — copyright controversy makes it toxic for any brand work. Stick to Runway or Veo
